International Relations: Unit 1

Indian Foreign Policy 2026:
Principles & Neighbouring Policy

Back to IR Dashboard

Indian Foreign Policy 2026 reflects India’s principles, objectives, and strategies in dealing with global and neighbouring countries. Rooted in Panchsheel, non-alignment, and national interest, it now focuses on multi-alignment, security, growth, climate action, and regional cooperation.

Indian Foreign Policy refers to the set of rules, principles, and decisions that govern how India engages with the international community. It reflects history, culture, economy, geography, and political values. Designed after independence in 1947, it aims to protect sovereignty, ensure security, promote development, and enhance India’s role in the world order.

In 2026, Indian foreign policy is more relevant than it has ever been. During this time, there are huge global challenges such as multipolar competition, climate change, digital transformation, and shifting trade dynamics, all of which demand clear strategies. Understanding the determinants, principles, and phases is essential for UPSC aspirants to analyse India’s global position.

What is Indian Foreign Policy?

It is India’s official strategy to manage relations with other countries and international organisations. It includes political, economic, defence, and cultural interactions, setting positions on global peace, terrorism, trade, and nuclear security.

Key Objectives:

  • Sovereignty: Safeguard sovereignty and territorial integrity as the foremost priority.
  • Economic Growth: Promote growth through proactive international cooperation.
  • Stability: Maintain peace and stability in the South Asian region.
  • Influence: Expand India’s influence in global governance frameworks.
  • Sustainability: Support sustainable development and climate responsibility.

Determinants of Foreign Policy 2026

Influenced by both domestic and international factors, these shape India's external decisions.

Internal Determinants

  • Geography: India shares a 3,488 km border with China and 3,323 km with Pakistan. History of wars (1962, 1947, 1965, 1971, 1999) makes border security vital. A 7,500 km coastline emphasizes Indian Ocean energy and trade security.
  • Historical Context: Freedom in 1947 led to solidarity with Asian/African nations. 1961 saw the birth of NAM to avoid siding with U.S. or USSR.
  • Economic Needs: Post-1991 reforms led to an open market. By 2026, India is the world's fastest-growing major economy (6.5%–6.6%), making tech/energy partnerships crucial.
  • Domestic Politics: States influence policy (e.g., West Bengal/Bangladesh water deals, Tamil Nadu/Sri Lanka Tamil issue).

External Determinants

During the Cold War (1947–1991), India stayed neutral between U.S. and USSR. By 2026, India balances ties with U.S., Russia, EU, Japan, and ASEAN while handling China's challenges.

Objectives and Principles

Core Objectives:

  • Sovereignty & Integrity: Foremost objective. Example: SCO Chairmanship where PM Modi emphasized respect for sovereignty.
  • Peace & Security: Advocacy for coexistence and dialogue. India is a significant contributor to UN Peacekeeping Forces.
  • Economic Well-being: Leveraged for tech transfer and growth. Example: CECA with Singapore and EPA with Japan.
  • Security Enhancement: Addressing traditional and non-traditional threats. Example: Restrictions on bidders from land-border-sharing countries.
  • Global Standing: Leadership in G20, SCO, and G7 (invitee) to shape international norms.
  • Indian Diaspora: Protecting the 17.5 million-strong diaspora. Example: Operations Devi Shakti, Ganga, and Kaveri.

Leadership Factors (The Evolution)

1947-64 Jawaharlal Nehru: Promoted peace, NAM, and signed Panchsheel in 1954.
1966-84 Indira Gandhi: 1971 Bangladesh Liberation and 1974 nuclear tests.
1998 A.B. Vajpayee: Nuclear tests (Pokhran-II) and strengthening U.S. ties.
Recent 2014-2026: Act East, Multi-alignment, Quad group, and global climate leadership.

Principles of Indian Foreign Policy 2026

  • Panchsheel: Mutual respect for integrity, non-aggression, non-interference, equality, and peaceful coexistence.
  • Non-alignment to Multi-alignment: Moving from staying away from power blocs to engaging with all major powers independently.
  • Peaceful Settlement: Choosing diplomacy and international law over armed conflict.
  • National Interest: Primacy of country safety and progress balanced with global fairness.

Policy with Neighbouring Countries (Neighbourhood First)

Eastern & Northern Ties

  • Bangladesh: Trade, power, connectivity; River-sharing (sensitive).
  • Nepal: Cultural ties, Hydropower; Political tensions.
  • Bhutan: Trusted partner, Hydropower focus.
  • Myanmar: Border security and connectivity emphasis.

Island & Western Ties

  • Sri Lanka: Support in recovery, infrastructure, Tamil reconciliation.
  • Maldives: Maritime security and infrastructure projects.
  • Afghanistan: Development projects; restricted by political changes.
  • Pakistan: Strained by terrorism and unresolved disputes.

Conclusion & Summary

By 2026, India has solidified its role as a Voice of the Global South. Transitioning from 1947's vulnerability to 2026's strategic autonomy, India now balances ties with the U.S., Russia, EU, and Japan simultaneously. Through policies like SAGAR and Act East, India maintains its unique strategy for growth while demanding reforms in the UN and WTO.
