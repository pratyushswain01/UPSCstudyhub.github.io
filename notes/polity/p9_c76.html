Part XII of the Constitution

Rights and Liabilities of the Government

Property, Contracts, and Legal Framework under the Constitution

Constitutional Framework (Articles 294-300)

Articles 294 to 300 detail the rights, liabilities, and responsibilities related to property, contracts, and legal suits involving the Union and State governments. This section governs the succession of assets, compulsory acquisition, and the legal personality of the State.

Property and Asset Succession

  • Succession: All property, assets, rights, and liabilities previously vested in the Dominion of India or princely states were transferred to the Union or corresponding States.
  • Escheat & Lapse: Property accruing to the State through lack of heirs (Escheat), disuse (Lapse), or lack of owner (Bona Vacantia) vests in the State or Union.
  • Sea-Wealth: All resources within territorial waters, the continental shelf, and the Exclusive Economic Zone vest exclusively in the Union.

Compulsory Acquisition of Property

Parliament and State legislatures are empowered to enact laws for the compulsory acquisition of private property. Notably, the 44th Amendment Act (1978) abolished the general obligation to pay compensation, except for:

Acquisition of property belonging to minority educational institutions.
Acquisition of land held under personal cultivation within statutory ceiling limits.

Suits by or Against the Government

  • Legal Entity: The Union of India and States are "juristic personalities." Lawsuits are filed in the name of the "Union of India" or the "State of [Name]."
  • Extent of Liability: Government liability is determined by pre-Constitution status unless superseded by new laws.
  • Sovereign vs. Non-Sovereign: The State cannot be sued for Torts (civil wrongs) committed during the exercise of sovereign functions (e.g., administering justice, military operations).

Contractual Liabilities

Contracts must fulfill three mandatory constitutional conditions to be binding:

  • Must be expressed as made by the President/Governor.
  • Must be executed on their behalf.
  • Must be executed by an authorized person in a prescribed manner.

Protection and Immunity (Article 361)

OfficialNature of Protection
President/GovernorAbsolute immunity for official acts; no criminal proceedings during tenure.
MinistersNo immunity for personal acts (can be sued for crimes/torts).
Judicial OfficersFull protection for official acts under the Judicial Officers Protection Act (1850).

Conclusion

The Constitution creates a robust framework balancing State authority with legal accountability. By delineating sovereign immunity from civil liability, and establishing clear protocols for contracts and property acquisition, the legal system ensures that the Indian state remains both powerful in administration and answerable under the law.
