Chapter 21: Cabinet Committees

Back to Main Menu

In India, Cabinet Committees are smaller groups of ministers formed by the Prime Minister from the larger Council of Ministers to address specific policy issues and make decisions on behalf of the government. These committees play a crucial role in ensuring effective decision-making, coordination, and implementation of policies across different sectors.

Features of Cabinet Committees

Established by the PM: The Prime Minister establishes these committees based on situational needs, so their number and composition can vary.
Extra-Constitutional: They are not mentioned in the Constitution but are provided for in the Rules of Business.
Organizational Purpose: They reduce the Cabinet's workload, allow for in-depth policy examination, and ensure effective coordination.
Types: Can be standing (permanent) or ad hoc (temporary for specific tasks).
Membership: Typically 3 to 8 members, mostly Cabinet Ministers, but others are not excluded. Senior ministers are often included for their expertise.
Leadership: Mostly chaired by the Prime Minister. If the PM is a member, he presides. Other senior ministers can also be chairmen.

Key Leadership Note

All Cabinet Committees, except Accommodation and Parliamentary Affairs, are headed by the Prime Minister. The Political Affairs Committee is considered the most powerful, often called a Super-Cabinet.

Functions of various Cabinet Committees

Cabinet Committee on Political Affairs (headed by PM):

Addresses issues on Centre-state relations and examines political matters that do not involve security.

Cabinet Committee on Economic Affairs (headed by PM):

Reviews economic trends, shapes economic policy, and deals with high-value investment proposals (> Rs 1,000 crore), agricultural pricing, and the Public Distribution System.

Appointments Committee of the Cabinet (headed by PM):

Appoints top military positions, decides on senior-level officer postings and empanelments in the Central government.

Cabinet Committee on Security (headed by PM):

Handles law and order, internal security, foreign policy matters with security implications, and defense expenditure over Rs 1,000 crore.

Cabinet Committee on Parliamentary Affairs (headed by Defence Minister):

Formulates Parliament session schedules and monitors the progress of government business.

Cabinet Committee on Accommodation (headed by Home Minister):

Determines guidelines and makes decisions on the allotment of government accommodation.

Cabinet Committee on Investment and Growth:

Identifies key projects for time-bound implementation involving investments of Rs 1,000 crore or more. Prescribes time limits for approvals and clearances, and monitors project progress.

Cabinet Committee on Employment and Skill Development:

Directs policies, programs, and initiatives for skill development to enhance workforce employability. It works towards closing gaps between skill availability and sector requirements, sets targets for speedy implementation, and periodically reviews progress.

Advantages

  • Facilitates in-depth policy examination and coordination.
  • Operates on the principle of division of labor for efficiency.
  • Safeguards the principle of collective responsibility.
  • Economises the Cabinet's valuable time.
  • Acts as a check on arbitrary actions by ministers.
  • Reduces the workload of Cabinet Ministers.

Critique

  • Sometimes established for trivial issues, wasting resources.
  • Junior ministers are seldom appointed, limiting diverse perspectives.
  • Meetings are not always held regularly, causing delays.
  • Memberships can be based on political needs rather than merit.
  • Over-reliance on the PM's involvement can create bottlenecks.
