State Public Service Commission (SPSC)

Back to Main Menu

On the lines of UPSC in the centre, there is a State Public Service Commission (SPSC) for every state, which is an independent Constitutional body. It serves as the major recruiting agency of a state, ensuring merit-based appointments under Articles 315 to 323 in Part XIV of the Constitution.

Evolution and Significance

  • Historical Roots: The Government of India Act, 1919 proposed a Federal Public Service Commission. Following the Lee Commission (1924) recommendations, it was established in 1926.
  • Statutory Recognition: The Government of India Act, 1935 provided for both the Central and State Public Service Commissions.
  • Independence: It is a constitutional watchdog of the merit system in the State, providing an impartial recruitment process.

Composition and Appointment (Article 316)

  • Appointment: The SPSC consists of a Chairman and other members appointed by the Governor of the state.
  • Qualifications: At least 50% of the members must have held office for a minimum of 10 years under either the Government of India or a State Government.
  • Tenure: Members hold office for a term of 6 years or until they attain the age of 62 years, whichever is earlier.
  • Acting Chairman: The Governor can appoint an acting chairman if the office is vacant or the chairman is unable to perform duties due to absence.

Removal and Suspension (Article 317)

Removal Authority: The President

While the Governor appoints SPSC members, they can only be removed by the President of India on specific grounds:

  • Insolvency (bankruptcy).
  • Engaging in paid employment outside the duties of the office.
  • Infirmity of mind or body.
  • Misbehaviour: Must be referred to the Supreme Court for an inquiry. The SC's advice is binding on the President.

Independence and Safeguards

  • Security of Tenure: Removal is only possible as per constitutional grounds, not at the pleasure of the Governor.
  • Financial Independence: Expenses, salaries, and pensions are charged on the Consolidated Fund of the State (not subject to legislative vote).
  • Employment Restrictions: After ceasing office, the Chairman is eligible to become the Chairman/Member of UPSC or Chairman of another SPSC, but ineligible for any other government job.

Functions and Advisory Role

Core Responsibilities

  • Conducting examinations for appointments to State Services.
  • Consultation: Advising on methods of recruitment, principles for appointments, promotions, and inter-service transfers.
  • Disciplinary Matters: Advising on disciplinary actions (censure, withholding of increments, removal, dismissal) affecting civil servants.
  • Pension Claims: Evaluating claims for injuries sustained while serving the State Government.

Note: SPSC recommendations are advisory and not binding on the Government. However, the Government must explain the reasons for non-acceptance to the State Legislature.

Limitations and Conflict of Roles

  • Exclusions: The SPSC is not consulted on reservations for backward classes or claims of SCs and STs in services.
  • Vigilance Conflict: The emergence of the State Vigilance Commission (SVC) in disciplinary matters has created a dual-consultation challenge, though SPSC (constitutional) holds an edge.
  • Legal Recourse: The Supreme Court ruled that failure to consult SPSC does not invalidate government decisions; the aggrieved servant has no legal remedy in court for this specific failure.

Joint State Public Service Commission (JSPSC)

Statutory Nature

The Constitution makes provision for a JSPSC to cater to the needs of two or more states. Unlike UPSC and SPSC, it is a Statutory Body (created by an Act of Parliament), not a Constitutional body.

  • Appointment: Members are appointed by the President.
  • Tenure: 6 years or age 62 (same as SPSC).
  • Reports: Submits annual reports to the Governors of each participating state.
  • Example: Punjab and Haryana had a JSPSC for a short period after the reorganization in 1966.
