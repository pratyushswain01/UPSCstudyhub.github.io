Part XIV of the Constitution

Public Services in India

Civil Services, Constitutional Framework, and Administrative Evolution

Definition and Scope

Public services (civil or government services) are divided into three categories: all-India services, central services, and state services. The permanent executive is another name for civil service. It is regarded as the backbone of a state apparatus.

Public service includes services provided by the government directly or via financing private provision, the interactions occurring from these, and grievance redressal mechanisms. It covers daily aspects like health, education, infrastructure, and law and order.

Historical Background

  • British Administration: Initially regulatory to expand British interests, later shifting to developmental roles.
  • Evolution of Training: Lord Wellesley established the College of Fort William (1800). Later, training moved to East India College, Haileybury, England.
  • Recruitment: Originally by Director's nomination. Following INC demands (1885), the curriculum was broadened and a parallel exam was introduced in India in 1922.

All India Services

All-India services are shared by both Central and state governments. Members hold key posts and serve both alternately.

Indian Administrative Service (IAS)
Indian Police Service (IPS)
Indian Forest Service (IFS)
  • Governance: The All-India Services Act of 1951 empowers the Centre to make rules in consultation with states.
  • Control: Jointly controlled, but only the Central government can take disciplinary action (impose penalties).
  • Father of Services: Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel is regarded as the architect of these services.

Significance of Public Services

  • Important tool for political modernization and ensuring stability/continuity of government.
  • Fosters sense of national belonging and promotes a welfare-oriented, inclusive society.
  • Ensures the Rule of Law by balancing political direction with legal mandates.

Criticism and Challenges

  • Inefficiency: Increased spending often fails to match outcomes due to corruption and red-tapism.
  • Systemic Issues: Excessive centralization, nepotism, and policies often removed from ground realities.
  • Politicization: Frequent mass transfers lead to bureaucrats seeking political patronage and favor.

Way Forward

To handle massive responsibilities, the bureaucracy must evolve:

  • Gain expertise in modern management, science, technology, and behavioural sciences.
  • Implement capability development programs tailored to specific local cultural needs.
  • Embrace flexibility in public sector enterprises.
  • Reform Agenda: Focus on recruitment transparency, behavioral changes, and strict anti-corruption laws.
